About Me

I am a Principal Investigator at the Shanghai Qi Zhi Institute. I received my Ph.D. from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, advised by Professor Jonathan P. How. During my doctoral studies, my research focused on reinforcement learning, robotic perception, and autonomous driving.

During and after my Ph.D., I served as a machine learning researcher at Meta Reality Lab and several startup companies in the autonomous driving and robotics sectors. In late 2023, I joined the Qi Zhi Institute full-time, where my research centers on embodied intelligence, with a particular focus on developing simple, unified, and principled algorithms for general embodied intelligence.

Research Direction

My research aims to advance embodied intelligence by developing simple and principled approaches that are scalable and generalizable to various embodied AI tasks and domains.

Recent Works

1) Neural SDEs as a Unified Approach to Continuous-Domain Sequence Modeling

We are developing a novel approach to modeling continuous sequence dynamics using Neural Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs). Inspired by the use of differential equations in science and engineering, this framework treats time-series data as samples from an underlying continuous system. By parameterizing the drift and diffusion terms with neural networks, we ensure scalability and adaptability. A key innovation is incorporating intrinsic uncertainty directly into the model, making it robust to scaling across different data modalities. This approach is showing promise across diverse tasks in embodied and generative AI, offering both simplicity and flexibility.

2) Denoising-based Contractive Imitation Learning

We are addressing the challenge of covariate shift in imitation learning through a simple yet powerful denoising mechanism. Our method leverages a dual-network design: a dynamics model predicts transitions, while a denoising policy network refines these predictions and guides actions. By enhancing stability and contraction properties in state transitions, our approach reduces reliance on additional expert data or complex adversarial setups. This work is paving the way for more robust imitation learning systems that thrive even under noise and perturbations.
