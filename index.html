About Me

I am a Principal Investigator at the Shanghai Qi Zhi Institute. I received my Ph.D. from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, advised by Professor Jonathan P. How. During my doctoral studies, my research focused on reinforcement learning, robotic perception, and autonomous driving, and I published multiple papers in top conferences and journals in the fields of robotics, machine learning, and autonomous driving.

During and after my Ph.D., I served as a machine learning researcher at Meta Reality Lab and several startup companies in the autonomous driving and robotics sectors. In late 2023, I joined the Qi Zhi Institute full-time, where my research centers on embodied intelligence, with a particular focus on developing simple, unified, and principled algorithms for general embodied intelligence.

Research Direction

My research aims to advance embodied intelligence through a simple, unified, and principled approach. I focus on algorithmic research that can generalize to various embodied AI tasks and domains.

Recent Works

1) Neural SDEs as a Unified Approach to Continuous-Domain Sequence Modeling

Inspired by the pervasive use of differential equations to model continuous dynamics across scientific and engineering domains, we propose a novel and intuitive approach to continuous sequence modeling. We interpret time-series data as discrete samples from an underlying continuous dynamical system and model its time evolution using a general form of Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE), where both the drift and diffusion terms are parameterized by neural networks.

We derive a principled maximum likelihood objective that aligns with the recently developed Flow Matching/Stochastic Interpolant framework but offers a key advantage: invariance under data scaling, achieved by explicitly learning the intrinsic data uncertainty captured by the diffusion term. This property alleviates the need for manual weight tuning across different modalities.

Experiments on a variety of sequence modeling tasks in embodied and generative AI demonstrate the versatility and robustness of Neural SDEs, showcasing improved performance and flexibility over existing methods.

2) Denoising-based Contractive Imitation Learning

A fundamental challenge in imitation learning is the problem of covariate shift. Existing methods to mitigate this often require additional expert interactions, access to environment dynamics, or complex adversarial training, which may not be practical in real-world applications.

We propose a simple yet effective method to address covariate shift by introducing a denoising mechanism that enhances the contraction properties of the state transition mapping. Our approach involves training two neural networks: a dynamics model \( f \) that predicts the next state from the current state, and a joint state-action denoising policy network \( d \) that refines this state prediction via denoising and outputs the corresponding action.

We theoretically prove that the denoising network acts as a local contraction mapping, reducing the Lipschitz constant of the state transition and improving stability. Our method can be easily integrated into existing imitation learning frameworks without requiring additional expert data or complex modifications. Empirical results demonstrate improved success rates in various imitation learning tasks under noise perturbations.
