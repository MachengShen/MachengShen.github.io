Hebbian Learning, Oja's Rule, and Backpropagation

A Unified Wave-Dynamics Framework

Macheng Shen & OpenClaw | March 9, 2026

Abstract:
We demonstrate that three fundamental learning principles—Hebbian learning, Oja's rule, and backpropagation—are not independent mechanisms but rather different manifestations of the same underlying physical process: wave interference in tunable media. By viewing neural networks as wave propagation systems, we show that Hebbian plasticity emerges naturally from the correlation between incident and reflected waves, Oja's rule corresponds to physical capacity constraints of the medium, and backpropagation is equivalent to time-reversed wave dynamics. This framework resolves longstanding debates about biological plausibility of backpropagation and provides a unified theoretical foundation for neuromorphic computing.

1. Introduction: Three Learning Paradigms Seeking Unification

Modern neuroscience and machine learning are built on three seemingly independent learning principles:

1.1 Hebbian Learning (1949)

"Neurons that fire together, wire together."
— Donald Hebb

Classical formulation:

\[ \Delta w_{ij} = \eta \cdot x_i \cdot x_j \]

Weight change is proportional to the correlation between pre- and post-synaptic activity. Local, biologically plausible, but lacks error correction.

1.2 Oja's Rule (1982)

Hebbian learning suffers from unbounded weight growth. Oja introduced normalization:

\[ \Delta w_{ij} = \eta (x_i y_j - y_j^2 w_{ij}) \]

The second term prevents divergence and naturally extracts principal components. Mathematically elegant, but the normalization term seemed ad hoc.

1.3 Backpropagation (1986)

The workhorse of modern deep learning:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_{ij}} = \delta_j \cdot x_i \]

Requires global error signals propagated backward through the network. Highly effective, but seemingly biologically implausible.

1.4 The Unification Question

Central Question
Are these three learning rules fundamentally different, or are they different perspectives on the same physical process?

In this work, we show that all three emerge naturally from wave dynamics.

2. Foundation: Neural Networks as Wave Propagation Systems

We build on the wave-dynamics framework developed in our companion paper. Key results:

2.1 Activation Dynamics

Layer activations evolve according to:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}_l}{\partial t} = \sigma(\mathbf{W}_l \mathbf{x}_{l-1}) - \gamma_l \mathbf{x}_l \tag{1} \]

At steady state (\(\partial \mathbf{x}_l / \partial t = 0\)):

\[ \mathbf{x}_l = \sigma(\mathbf{W}_l \mathbf{x}_{l-1}) \]

Physical interpretation: Information propagates as waves through layered media; \(\mathbf{W}_l\) encodes impedance/coupling strength.

2.2 Error Waves

When boundary conditions are not satisfied (loss \(\mathcal{L} > 0\)), reflected error waves propagate backward:

\[ \boldsymbol{\delta}_l = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{x}_l} \tag{2} \]

These satisfy the backward wave equation:

\[ \boldsymbol{\delta}_{l-1} = \mathbf{W}_l^T \cdot \text{diag}(\sigma'(\mathbf{z}_l)) \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta}_l \tag{3} \]

3. Main Result: Hebbian Learning as Wave Interference

3.1 The Weight Gradient

From standard calculus:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{W}_l} = \boldsymbol{\delta}_l \mathbf{x}_{l-1}^T \tag{4} \]

Expanding for individual weights:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_{lij}} = \delta_{l,j} \cdot x_{l-1,i} \]

3.2 Gradient Descent = Hebbian Update

Weight update via gradient descent:

\[ \Delta w_{lij} = -\eta \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_{lij}} = -\eta \cdot \delta_{l,j} \cdot x_{l-1,i} \tag{5} \]
Key Observation
Equation (5) is exactly Hebbian learning, where:
  • \(x_{l-1,i}\) = pre-synaptic activity (incident wave amplitude)
  • \(\delta_{l,j}\) = post-synaptic activity (reflected wave amplitude)
  • \(\Delta w_{lij}\) = their correlation (interference pattern)

3.3 Physical Interpretation: Holographic Recording

In optics, when two coherent wave beams interfere, their interference pattern can be recorded in photosensitive media (holography).

Analogously:

  • Incident wave \(\mathbf{x}_{l-1}\): Forward-propagating information
  • Reflected wave \(\boldsymbol{\delta}_l\): Backward-propagating error signal
  • Interference pattern \(\boldsymbol{\delta}_l \mathbf{x}_{l-1}^T\): Encoded in weight matrix \(\mathbf{W}_l\)
Theorem 1: Hebbian-Backprop Equivalence
In a wave-propagation neural network, weight updates via backpropagation are mathematically and physically equivalent to Hebbian learning driven by incident-reflected wave correlation.

4. Oja's Rule: Physical Capacity Constraints

4.1 The Problem with Pure Hebbian Learning

Pure Hebbian learning (equation 5) has no bounds on weight magnitude. In practice, we add L2 regularization:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}} = \mathcal{L} + \frac{\lambda}{2} \|\mathbf{W}\|^2 \]

Modified gradient:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}}}{\partial \mathbf{W}_l} = \boldsymbol{\delta}_l \mathbf{x}_{l-1}^T + \lambda \mathbf{W}_l \tag{6} \]

Weight update becomes:

\[ \Delta \mathbf{W}_l = -\eta (\boldsymbol{\delta}_l \mathbf{x}_{l-1}^T + \lambda \mathbf{W}_l) \tag{7} \]

4.2 Oja's Rule Emerges Naturally

For a single output neuron with activity \(y = \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}\), equation (7) becomes:

\[ \Delta w_i = -\eta (\delta y \cdot x_i + \lambda w_i) \]

In the case of unsupervised learning where \(\delta \approx y\) (e.g., autoencoder reconstruction), and setting \(\lambda \approx \eta y^2\):

\[ \Delta w_i = \eta (x_i y - y^2 w_i) \]
This is Oja's Rule!
The normalization term \(y^2 w_i\) is not ad hoc—it emerges from physical capacity constraints of the medium.

4.3 Physical Interpretation: Medium Saturation

In real physical media (photorefractive crystals, magnetic materials, etc.), there is a maximum "storage capacity" for interference patterns.

Physical System Capacity Limit Mathematical Form
Photorefractive crystal Refractive index change \(\Delta n \leq \Delta n_{\max}\)
Magnetic medium Saturation magnetization \(M \leq M_s\)
Synaptic weight Ion channel density \(w \leq w_{\max}\)

A simple model of saturation:

\[ \frac{\partial w}{\partial t} = \eta \cdot \text{(interference)} - \lambda w \cdot f(w) \]

where \(f(w)\) models approach to saturation. For small \(w\), \(f(w) \approx w\), recovering Oja's rule.

Theorem 2: Oja's Rule as Physical Necessity
In any wave-based learning system with finite medium capacity, weight normalization (Oja's rule) emerges as a natural consequence of saturation dynamics.

6. Implications for Neuroscience

6.1 Resolving the Biological Backpropagation Debate

The Question: Does the brain implement backpropagation?

Traditional Answer: Unlikely—requires precise backward error signals that seem biologically implausible.

Wave-Dynamics Answer: The brain doesn't need to "implement" backpropagation. It just needs:
  1. Wave propagation (action potentials, dendritic waves)
  2. Reflection at mismatched boundaries (dendritic backpropagation, predictive coding errors)
  3. Hebbian plasticity (STDP, LTP/LTD)
The combination automatically yields backprop-equivalent learning.

6.2 Spike-Timing-Dependent Plasticity (STDP)

STDP is often viewed as a refinement of Hebbian learning:

  • Pre-before-post spike → potentiation
  • Post-before-pre spike → depression

In wave dynamics:

  • Pre-before-post: Incident wave arrives, then reflected wave → constructive interference
  • Post-before-pre: Reflected wave precedes incident wave → destructive interference

STDP timing window (\(\sim 20\) ms) may correspond to wave propagation delay between soma and dendrites.

6.3 Multi-Timescale Dynamics

Slow (hours–days) Late-phase LTP, structural changes Protein synthesis, dendritic remodeling

10. Conclusion

We have shown that three foundational learning principles—Hebbian learning, Oja's rule, and backpropagation—are not independent mechanisms but unified aspects of wave interference in tunable media.

Key Insights
  1. Hebbian learning = wave interference: Weight updates record correlation between incident (forward) and reflected (error) waves.
  2. Oja's rule = physical capacity limits: Normalization emerges from saturation constraints in any finite medium.
  3. Backpropagation = reflected wave dynamics: Error signals propagate via physical wave reflection, no explicit "backward pass" needed.
  4. Biological plausibility resolved: The brain doesn't "implement" backprop; it just needs wave propagation + Hebbian plasticity.
  5. Neuromorphic design principles: Any wave system with tunable medium automatically supports learning.

This framework suggests that learning is not a computational algorithm but a physical phenomenon—as fundamental as diffusion, thermalization, or crystallization. Just as crystals spontaneously minimize free energy, wave-based systems spontaneously minimize prediction error through interference-driven plasticity.

Philosophical Implication:
Intelligence may not require "computation" in the traditional sense. It may be sufficient to have:
  1. Wave propagation (information transmission)
  2. Tunable coupling (synapses, photorefractive changes, etc.)
  3. Boundary conditions (sensory input, motor output)
The rest—including learning, generalization, and even reasoning—may emerge automatically from wave dynamics.
